# John Snow

## Question 1: Correlation vs. Causation

The map showed a high concentration of deaths near the Broad Street pump. However, proximity alone only proves a correlation. What specific steps did Snow take to move his argument from correlation (deaths happen near the pump) to causation (the pump water is causing the deaths)?

**Answer:**

Snow gathered detailed evidence instead of relying only on the pattern on the map. He interviewed families, checked where victims lived, and traced which water source they actually used. He also looked for exceptions, such as people who lived far away but drank from the Broad Street pump, and people nearby who stayed healthy because they used different water. Those details helped rule out other explanations and supported the claim that contaminated pump water was causing the deaths.
## Question 2: The Danger of Missing Data

Imagine if Snow had only collected data from the local hospitals, rather than walking the streets and knocking on doors. How would this have changed his dataset, and how might it have altered his conclusions?

**Answer:**

If Snow had only used hospital records, his data would have been incomplete and less accurate. He might have missed people who died at home, missed details about where they got their water, and missed unusual cases that challenged simple assumptions. That would have weakened his analysis and possibly made him less confident that the Broad Street pump was the true source of the outbreak. By collecting data directly from households, he built a much stronger and more reliable dataset.
## Question 3: The "Miasma" of Today

The "miasma theory" was a widely accepted consensus that was fundamentally incorrect but seemed to explain the surface-level facts (Soho smelled bad, and people in Soho were dying). What is a modern example of a "miasma" - a widely accepted assumption in business, society, or science that might fall apart if rigorously tested with data?

**Answer:**

A modern example could be the belief that working in the office always leads to better productivity. This assumption seems believable because managers can see employees in person, just as people in Snow's time connected bad smells with disease. But careful data might show that productivity depends more on the kind of work, communication quality, and team structure than on physical location alone. Like miasma theory, it is an explanation that sounds convincing on the surface but may fail under stronger evidence.
